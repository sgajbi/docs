# 01 - Bonds Fundamentals and Product Taxonomy

## 1. What Is a Bond?

A **bond** is a debt security. The issuer borrows money from investors and promises to pay interest and repay principal according to agreed terms.

At the simplest level:

| Role | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Issuer | Borrower. This may be a government, company, agency, municipality, supranational, or financial institution. |
| Investor / bondholder | Lender. The investor buys the bond and receives contractual cashflows. |
| Principal / face value / par | Amount the issuer promises to repay at maturity, unless default or another event occurs. |
| Coupon | Interest paid by the issuer. |
| Maturity date | Date when the bond principal is due to be repaid. |
| Price | Market value of the bond, usually quoted as a percentage of par. |
| Yield | Return implied by price and expected cashflows. |

Example:

| Feature | Example |
|---|---:|
| Face value | USD 100,000 |
| Issuer | Company ABC |
| Coupon | 5% p.a. |
| Coupon frequency | Semi-annual |
| Maturity | 5 years |
| Issue price | 100% of par |

Investor receives USD 2,500 every six months and USD 100,000 principal at maturity, assuming no default.

## 2. Bond vs Note vs Deposit vs Loan vs Bond Fund

| Product | Simplified Meaning | Client Perception | Key Difference |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bond | Debt security issued into capital markets | Fixed-income investment | Tradable security with market price and yield. |
| Note | Debt instrument, often medium-term or issued under a note programme | Fixed income or structured product | In private banking, often means structured note. |
| Deposit | Money placed with a bank | Cash/deposit product | May have deposit protection depending on jurisdiction; usually simpler. |
| Loan | Direct lending arrangement | Private credit / lending | Usually bilateral or syndicated, not always security-like for retail portfolios. |
| Bond fund / ETF | Fund holding many bonds | Diversified fixed-income exposure | Investor owns fund units, not the underlying bonds directly. |

Important distinction:

```text
If the client owns an individual bond, the position has a maturity, issuer, coupon, price, accrued interest, and principal repayment profile.
If the client owns a bond fund, the client owns fund units; the fund manages many bonds internally.
```

## 3. Why Bonds Exist

Issuers use bonds to raise financing.

| Issuer Objective | Example |
|---|---|
| Fund government spending | Treasury / sovereign bonds |
| Finance corporate expansion | Corporate bonds |
| Refinance existing debt | New bond issued to repay maturing debt |
| Fund infrastructure | Municipal / agency / project bonds |
| Match long-term liabilities | Financial institutions issuing senior or subordinated debt |
| Package asset cashflows | Asset-backed securities / mortgage-backed securities |

Investors buy bonds for income, capital preservation, diversification, liability matching, yield, credit exposure, duration exposure, currency exposure, or tactical rate/spread views.

## 4. Core Bond Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Face value / par / principal | Amount used to calculate coupon and redemption. |
| Nominal amount | Amount of face value held by the investor. |
| Coupon rate | Annual interest rate applied to face value. |
| Coupon frequency | How often coupons are paid: annual, semi-annual, quarterly, monthly. |
| Issue date | Date the bond is issued. |
| Settlement date | Date cash/security settlement occurs. |
| Maturity date | Date principal is due to be repaid. |
| Clean price | Bond price excluding accrued interest. |
| Dirty price | Bond price including accrued interest. |
| Accrued interest | Interest earned since last coupon date but not yet paid. |
| Yield to maturity | Discount rate that equates bond price to future cashflows if held to maturity. |
| Yield to call | Yield assuming the bond is called on a call date. |
| Yield to worst | Lowest yield among possible redemption scenarios. |
| Duration | Sensitivity of bond price to yield/interest-rate movement. |
| DV01 / PV01 | Approximate price change for 1 basis point yield movement. |
| Credit spread | Extra yield over a risk-free or government benchmark. |
| Seniority | Priority of repayment in default. |
| Rating | Credit quality assessment by rating agencies or internal credit model. |

## 5. Bond Price, Coupon, and Yield: Simple Intuition

A bond's coupon is fixed by the contract, but its market price can change.

| Situation | Example | Interpretation |
|---|---:|---|
| Price at par | 100 | Bond trades at face value. |
| Price below par | 95 | Bond trades at discount; yield is usually higher than coupon. |
| Price above par | 105 | Bond trades at premium; yield is usually lower than coupon. |

Mental model:

```text
When market yields rise, prices of existing fixed-rate bonds usually fall.
When market yields fall, prices of existing fixed-rate bonds usually rise.
```

This inverse relationship is one of the most important fixed-income concepts.

## 6. Major Bond Categories by Issuer

### 6.1 Government / sovereign bonds

Issued by national governments.

Examples:

| Region | Examples |
|---|---|
| United States | Treasury bills, notes, bonds, TIPS |
| Singapore | Singapore Government Securities, Treasury bills, Singapore Savings Bonds |
| United Kingdom | Gilts |
| Germany | Bunds |
| Japan | JGBs |

Characteristics:

| Feature | Typical Treatment |
|---|---|
| Credit risk | Usually lower for strong sovereigns issuing in own currency, but not zero. |
| Liquidity | Often high for benchmark government bonds. |
| Use in portfolios | Defensive allocation, duration exposure, collateral, liquidity management. |
| Valuation | Market price / yield curve. |

### 6.2 Supranational bonds

Issued by institutions such as development banks or multinational agencies.

Examples: World Bank, Asian Development Bank, European Investment Bank.

Often perceived as high quality, but still require issuer and currency analysis.

### 6.3 Agency bonds

Issued by government-related entities or agencies. Credit support can vary significantly by jurisdiction and issuer.

### 6.4 Municipal bonds

Issued by states, cities, municipalities, or local government entities. In some jurisdictions, tax treatment is a major feature.

### 6.5 Corporate bonds

Issued by companies.

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Investment-grade corporate | Higher credit quality, lower yield. |
| High-yield corporate | Lower credit quality, higher yield, higher default risk. |
| Financial institution debt | Senior, subordinated, Tier 2, AT1, covered bonds, etc. |
| Industrial corporate debt | Issued by non-financial companies. |

## 7. Major Bond Categories by Coupon Structure

### 7.1 Fixed-rate bond

Pays a fixed coupon rate.

Example:

```text
5% p.a. paid semi-annually until maturity.
```

Most intuitive and common.

### 7.2 Floating-rate note / floating-rate bond

Coupon resets periodically based on a reference rate plus spread.

Example:

```text
Coupon = 3-month SOFR + 1.25%
```

Usually has lower interest-rate duration than a comparable fixed-rate bond, but still has credit and spread risk.

### 7.3 Zero-coupon bond

Pays no periodic coupon. Issued or traded at a discount and repays par at maturity.

Example:

```text
Buy at 80, receive 100 at maturity.
```

High sensitivity to rates because all cashflow is at maturity.

### 7.4 Step-up / step-down coupon bond

Coupon changes according to a predefined schedule.

Example:

| Period | Coupon |
|---|---:|
| Year 1 | 3% |
| Year 2 | 4% |
| Year 3 | 5% |

### 7.5 Inflation-linked bond

Principal and/or coupon is linked to inflation index.

Examples: US TIPS, some inflation-linked sovereign bonds.

---

## 8. Major Bond Categories by Embedded Feature

### 8.1 Callable bond

Issuer has the right to redeem the bond before maturity on specified dates and prices.

Investor risk:

```text
If rates fall, issuer may call the bond and investor has to reinvest at lower yields.
```

### 8.2 Putable bond

Investor has the right to sell the bond back to issuer at specified dates/prices.

This can reduce investor downside in some scenarios but may offer lower yield.

### 8.3 Convertible bond

Bondholder can convert the bond into shares according to conversion terms.

Combined economics:

| Component | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Bond floor | Debt value from coupon/principal. |
| Equity option | Right to convert into shares. |
| Credit risk | Issuer may default. |
| Equity sensitivity | Value rises/falls with underlying share. |

### 8.4 Exchangeable bond

Similar to convertible, but conversion is into shares of another company, not necessarily the issuer.

### 8.5 Perpetual bond

No fixed maturity date. Often callable. Common in financial institution capital instruments and corporate hybrids.

Important:

```text
Perpetual does not mean risk-free income forever.
There may be call risk, extension risk, coupon deferral risk, subordination risk, and loss-absorption risk.
```

### 8.6 Sinking fund / amortising bond

Principal is repaid gradually according to a schedule.

## 9. Major Bond Categories by Security / Seniority

| Type | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Secured bond | Backed by specific collateral. |
| Unsecured senior bond | Senior claim, but no specific collateral. |
| Subordinated bond | Lower priority than senior debt. |
| Tier 2 capital | Bank regulatory capital instrument; subordinated. |
| Additional Tier 1 / AT1 | Deeply subordinated bank capital; may have coupon cancellation and write-down/conversion risk. |
| Covered bond | Bond backed by issuer and a cover pool of assets, common in some markets. |
| Asset-backed security | Cashflows backed by a pool of financial assets. |
| Mortgage-backed security | Cashflows backed by mortgage pool. |

Seniority matters because it affects recovery in default.

## 11. Why Clients Buy Bonds

| Objective | How Bonds Help |
|---|---|
| Income | Coupons provide periodic cashflow. |
| Capital preservation | Higher-quality bonds can reduce portfolio volatility. |
| Diversification | Bonds may behave differently from equities. |
| Liability matching | Maturities can align to future cash needs. |
| Liquidity reserve | Government/T-bills may serve as liquid allocation. |
| Yield pickup | Credit, tenor, currency, and structure can increase yield. |
| Tactical rate view | Client can express view on interest rates and duration. |
| Credit view | Client can express view on issuer/sector credit quality. |
| Currency view | Foreign-currency bonds add FX exposure. |

Important mental model:

```text
Higher yield usually means the investor is accepting more risk: longer duration, weaker credit, subordination, currency risk, liquidity risk, call/extension risk, or structural complexity.
```

## 12. Main Risks of Bonds

| Risk | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Interest-rate risk | Fixed-rate bond prices usually fall when market yields rise. |
| Duration risk | Longer-duration bonds are more sensitive to rate changes. |
| Credit/default risk | Issuer may fail to pay coupon or principal. |
| Spread risk | Credit spreads can widen even without default, reducing price. |
| Liquidity risk | Some bonds may be hard to sell at fair price. |
| Reinvestment risk | Coupons or redeemed principal may need reinvestment at lower yield. |
| Call risk | Issuer may redeem early when it benefits issuer. |
| Extension risk | Bond may not be called when investor expected it to be called. |
| Prepayment risk | ABS/MBS principal may return earlier than expected. |
| Currency risk | Foreign-currency bonds move with FX rates. |
| Inflation risk | Fixed coupon loses purchasing power if inflation rises. |
| Subordination risk | Lower-ranking bonds may recover less in default. |
| Event risk | M&A, downgrade, regulation, litigation, or capital structure change. |
| Tax risk | Coupon, discount, capital gains, withholding tax treatment may vary. |
| Documentation risk | Terms may include call, deferral, write-down, conversion, or negative covenants. |

## 13. Bond vs Structured Note: Key Difference

| Dimension | Plain Bond | Structured Note |
|---|---|---|
| Core economic form | Debt security | Debt security plus derivative payoff |
| Coupon | Usually fixed/floating contractual coupon | May be fixed, conditional, memory, digital, range-based, etc. |
| Principal repayment | Usually par at maturity unless default/call/etc. | May depend on market condition or underlying asset. |
| Underlying asset | Usually none, except issuer credit/collateral | Equity, index, FX, rate, credit, commodity, basket, etc. |
| Valuation | DCF / yield / spread / market price | Bond value + derivative value, often model-heavy. |
| Lifecycle | Coupon, call, maturity, default | Observation, barrier, autocall, conversion, payoff formula, etc. |

A bond can be complex, but a structured note usually has more payoff complexity.

---

## 14. Practitioner Summary

A bond is a debt security where the investor lends money to an issuer in exchange for coupon payments and principal repayment at maturity. The key variables are issuer, seniority, coupon, maturity, currency, price, yield, duration, credit rating, and embedded features such as call, put, conversion, amortisation, or inflation linkage. Bonds are used for income, diversification, capital preservation, liability matching, and tactical views on rates or credit. The major risks are interest-rate risk, credit/default risk, spread risk, liquidity risk, reinvestment risk, call/extension risk, currency risk, inflation risk, and subordination risk. From a platform perspective, the bond should be modelled as a security position primarily held by nominal amount, with separate schedules for coupons, calls, puts, amortisation, ratings, cashflows, lifecycle events, and valuations.
