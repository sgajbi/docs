# 01 - Commodities Fundamentals, Taxonomy and Wealth Context

## 1. What are commodities?

Commodities are raw materials or primary goods that are produced, mined, extracted, grown or harvested and can be traded in physical or financial markets.

Unlike equities, commodities do not represent ownership of a business. Unlike bonds, they do not usually generate contractual coupon income. Their return is mainly driven by price movement, scarcity, supply-demand conditions, storage economics, financing cost, inventory levels, geopolitical events, weather, transport constraints and currency effects.

For wealth management, commodities are usually not held because the client wants to consume the physical commodity. They are held to achieve one or more portfolio objectives:

- diversification;
- inflation sensitivity;
- geopolitical or crisis hedge;
- tactical macro exposure;
- currency debasement hedge;
- real-asset allocation;
- portfolio insurance or tail-risk offset;
- commodity-cycle participation;
- collateral or liquidity management, especially in gold.

## 2. Commodity families

| Family | Examples | Main drivers | Typical access routes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Precious metals | Gold, silver, platinum, palladium | Real rates, USD, inflation expectations, central-bank demand, investment demand, jewellery/industrial demand | Physical, metal account, ETF/ETC/ETN, futures, options, structured products |
| Energy | Crude oil, refined products, natural gas, power | OPEC policy, geopolitics, inventory, weather, transport, refining margins, demand growth | Futures, options, swaps, ETPs, structured products, energy equities/funds |
| Industrial metals | Copper, aluminium, nickel, zinc, lead, tin, iron ore | Global growth, China demand, supply disruption, inventory, industrial cycle, electrification | Futures, forwards, swaps, mining equities, funds, structured products |
| Agriculture | Wheat, corn, soybeans, coffee, sugar, cotton, cocoa | Weather, harvest, seasonality, inventory, policy, logistics | Futures/options, commodity funds, ETPs, structured products |
| Livestock | Cattle, hogs | Feed cost, disease, demand, supply cycles | Futures/options, funds/ETPs in limited cases |
| Emissions and environmental markets | Carbon credits, allowances, renewable certificates | Regulation, policy, cap-and-trade design, compliance demand | Futures, forwards, specialist funds, certificates |
| Freight and shipping | Freight indices, dry bulk | Global trade, shipping capacity, fuel cost | Futures, swaps, specialist products |

## 3. Commodity exposure routes

The most important platform concept is that a commodity is the **underlying exposure**, not necessarily the accounting product.

| Wrapper | Client position type | Income? | Main extra risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Physical bullion | Metal holding / custody asset | No natural income | Storage, insurance, custody, bid/ask, authenticity |
| Allocated metal account | Specific metal credited to client | No natural income | Custody/operational risk, storage cost |
| Unallocated metal account | Claim on provider for metal amount | No natural income | Provider credit risk |
| Commodity ETF | Fund/ETF units | Usually no natural income for physical metal ETFs | Tracking, fees, liquidity, fund structure |
| Commodity ETC/ETN/certificate | Exchange-traded debt/security | Usually no natural income | Issuer credit, tracking, early redemption |
| Futures | Derivative position | No coupon; collateral may earn interest | Leverage, margin, roll, basis, expiry |
| Options | Derivative position | Premium-based | Volatility, time decay, exercise, leverage |
| Forwards/swaps | OTC derivative | Contractual payoff | Counterparty risk, collateral, valuation model |
| Structured note | Note position | Coupon may be conditional/fixed | Issuer risk, barrier/payoff complexity |
| Commodity equity/fund | Equity/fund position | Possible dividends/distributions | Equity/business risk, not pure commodity risk |

## 4. Why commodities behave differently from financial assets

Commodities are heavily influenced by physical-market constraints.

Key concepts:

| Concept | Meaning | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Spot price | Price for near-term delivery | Used for physical and short-dated exposure |
| Futures price | Price for delivery at future date | Used for derivatives and futures-based ETPs |
| Forward curve | Set of future delivery prices | Determines roll yield and valuation |
| Contango | Futures price above spot/near price | Rolling long futures can lose value if selling low and buying high |
| Backwardation | Futures price below spot/near price | Rolling long futures can gain value if selling high and buying low |
| Storage cost | Cost to store commodity | Raises forward price for storable commodities |
| Convenience yield | Benefit of holding physical inventory | Can lower forward prices and create backwardation |
| Carry | Financing + storage - convenience yield | Drives theoretical forward pricing |
| Basis | Difference between cash/spot and futures price | Critical for hedging and tracking |
| Roll yield | Gain/loss from replacing expiring futures | Major driver of futures-based commodity product returns |

## 5. Commodity return components

For physical gold, return is mostly price return minus custody/storage/spread costs.

For a futures-based commodity product, return is usually decomposed as:

```text
Total Return = Spot Return + Roll Return + Collateral Return - Fees - Tracking Error
```

For a commodity equity fund, return is not pure commodity return. It includes:

```text
Commodity Equity Return = Commodity Price Sensitivity + Company Earnings + Equity Market Beta + Dividends + FX + Management Quality
```

This matters for advisory and reporting. A gold miner is not the same as gold. An oil company is not the same as oil. A futures-based oil ETP is not the same as physical oil.

## 6. Wealth-management use cases

| Use case | Product route | Important caveat |
|---|---|---|
| Defensive gold allocation | Physical gold, gold ETF/ETC | No income; valuation volatility; custody/issuer risk depends on wrapper |
| Inflation hedge | Broad commodity index, energy/metals exposure | Inflation relationship is unstable and time-varying |
| Tactical oil view | Futures, options, ETP, structured note | Roll risk and volatility can dominate spot view |
| Industrial-cycle exposure | Copper/industrial metals ETP or mining equities | Mining equities add equity and company risk |
| Portfolio diversifier | Gold/broad commodities | Correlation may change during stress |
| Yield enhancement | Commodity-linked note | Yield comes from embedded option/issuer risk |
| Liquidity/collateral | Gold, short-term commodity-linked collateral arrangements | Haircuts, custody and eligibility rules matter |
| DPM real-asset allocation | Gold ETF, commodity fund, real-asset fund | Mandate must define allowed wrappers and limits |

## 7. Common misconceptions

| Misconception | Better understanding |
|---|---|
| Commodities always protect against inflation | They can help in some inflation regimes, but are volatile and not reliable in every period |
| Gold is risk-free | Gold has no credit risk if physically allocated, but has price, liquidity, custody and spread risk |
| A commodity ETF always tracks spot commodity price | Futures-based funds can materially deviate due to roll yield and collateral return |
| Commodity equities are commodity exposure | They are equity exposure with commodity sensitivity |
| Physical commodity value is simple | Custody, quality, storage location, fineness, assaying and delivery rules matter |
| Contango/backwardation are academic | They directly affect client returns in futures-based products |
| Listed means liquid | Some ETCs/ETNs/certificates may be listed but still have wide spreads or issuer market-making dependency |

## 8. Interview-ready explanation

Commodities are real assets or raw materials such as gold, oil, copper and agricultural goods. In wealth management, clients usually access commodities through physical holdings, precious metal accounts, ETFs/ETCs/ETNs, futures/options, swaps, funds, commodity-linked notes or commodity equities. The same underlying commodity can have very different lifecycle, valuation and risk depending on the wrapper. Physical gold requires custody and valuation controls; futures-based products require roll, margin and expiry handling; ETNs introduce issuer credit risk; commodity-linked notes introduce payoff complexity; and commodity equities add company-specific equity risk. From a platform perspective, it is important to model the legal product position separately from the look-through commodity exposure.
