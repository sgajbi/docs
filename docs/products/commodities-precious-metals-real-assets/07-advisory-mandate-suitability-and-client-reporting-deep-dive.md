# 07 - Advisory, Mandate, Suitability and Client Reporting Deep Dive

## 1. Advisory role of commodities

Commodities are usually used for portfolio objectives rather than income generation.

Common advisory narratives:

| Objective | Commodity use | Important caveat |
|---|---|---|
| Diversification | Gold/broad commodities may diversify equities/bonds | Correlation can change under stress |
| Inflation sensitivity | Energy/metals/agriculture can respond to price shocks | Inflation hedge is not stable or guaranteed |
| Crisis hedge | Gold may benefit in risk-off regimes | Gold can also fall when liquidity demand rises |
| Tactical macro view | Oil/copper/commodity index exposure | Timing and product wrapper matter |
| USD weakness hedge | Gold/commodities often USD-linked | Relationship is imperfect |
| Real-asset allocation | Commodity/real asset sleeve | Must distinguish commodities from real estate/infrastructure |
| Yield enhancement | Commodity-linked structured products | Yield comes from embedded risk, not natural income |
| Collateral | Gold may support lending in some frameworks | Haircut, custody and eligibility rules apply |

## 2. Suitability considerations

Before recommending a commodity product, advisors should assess:

| Area | Question |
|---|---|
| Client risk profile | Can the client tolerate commodity volatility? |
| Product knowledge | Does the client understand physical/ETP/derivative/structured wrapper differences? |
| Liquidity need | Can client exit easily or is there issuer/custody dependency? |
| Concentration | Is commodity exposure too large relative to portfolio? |
| Currency | Is exposure USD-denominated or FX-hedged? |
| Income need | Does client mistakenly expect income from gold/commodities? |
| Leverage | Does the product create leveraged notional exposure? |
| Margin | Could client face margin calls? |
| Counterparty | Is there issuer/provider/custodian risk? |
| Complexity | Is the product classified as complex/SIP? |
| Holding period | Is the product intended for short-term tactical use or long-term allocation? |
| Tax/accounting | Are there jurisdiction-specific tax or reporting issues? |

## 3. DPM and mandate treatment

Commodities must be explicitly defined in mandate rules.

Recommended mandate dimensions:

| Rule type | Example |
|---|---|
| Asset class eligibility | Commodities allowed up to 5% or prohibited |
| Wrapper eligibility | Physical gold allowed; futures prohibited; ETFs allowed |
| Derivative usage | Futures/options only for hedging, not leverage |
| Concentration limit | Max X% gold, Y% single commodity |
| Issuer limit | Max ETN/certificate issuer exposure |
| Counterparty limit | OTC commodity swap counterparty limit |
| Liquidity rule | Only daily-liquid exchange-traded products allowed |
| Leverage rule | No leveraged/inverse ETPs unless mandate permits |
| ESG rule | Exclude certain commodities or require responsible sourcing |
| Collateral rule | Gold collateral allowed only if bank-controlled custody |
| Reporting rule | Look-through commodity exposure required |

## 4. Advisory guidance by product wrapper

| Wrapper | Advisory message |
|---|---|
| Physical gold | Direct metal exposure but storage, insurance, spread and custody matter |
| Allocated gold account | Stronger ownership/custody claim than unallocated, but still custody and fee considerations |
| Unallocated gold account | Convenient but introduces provider credit risk |
| Gold ETF | Convenient listed exposure; check whether physically backed and understand fees/tracking |
| Commodity futures fund | Exposure can differ from spot due to roll yield and collateral return |
| Commodity ETN | Exposes client to issuer credit risk in addition to commodity risk |
| Leveraged commodity ETP | Usually suitable only for sophisticated/tactical investors; daily reset risk |
| Commodity-linked note | Coupon/payoff depends on embedded options and issuer solvency |
| Commodity equity fund | Equity risk, business risk and commodity sensitivity; not pure commodity exposure |

## 5. Portfolio construction

Commodities should be evaluated for:

- expected return contribution;
- volatility and drawdown impact;
- correlation with equity, fixed income and alternatives;
- inflation regime sensitivity;
- currency exposure;
- liquidity and rebalancing cost;
- stress behavior;
- mandate suitability;
- client psychological tolerance.

### Example allocation framework

| Portfolio type | Possible treatment |
|---|---|
| Conservative income portfolio | Small or no direct commodity; gold only if suitable; avoid leveraged products |
| Balanced portfolio | Small diversifier allocation through liquid gold/commodity funds |
| Growth portfolio | Tactical commodity exposure may be permitted |
| HNW/UHNW alternatives portfolio | Broader real-asset sleeve including commodities, real estate, infrastructure, private natural resources |
| DPM mandate | Rule-based exposure limits and approved instrument list |

## 6. Reporting requirements

Client reports should avoid hiding commodity complexity inside generic alternatives.

Recommended report sections:

| Report section | Commodity-specific content |
|---|---|
| Holdings | Product name, wrapper, quantity, price, market value |
| Asset allocation | Commodity family and wrapper split |
| Performance | Price return, FX return, fees, roll contribution if available |
| Risk | Volatility, VaR, drawdown, concentration, issuer/counterparty risk |
| Income | Clarify no natural income unless distribution/coupon exists |
| Liquidity | Exchange-traded, issuer bid, physical custody, OTC |
| Collateral | Pledged and available commodity value |
| Notes/disclosures | Contango/roll risk, issuer risk, storage/custody risk, leverage |

| Holding | Wrapper | Commodity exposure | MV | Notional | Liquidity | Key risk |
|---|---|---|---:|---:|---|---|

## 8. Advisory red flags

| Red flag | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Client believes gold cannot lose value | Gold price can fall materially |
| Client seeks income from commodities | Most commodities do not generate income |
| Large tactical oil product allocation | High volatility and roll risk |
| Leveraged/inverse commodity ETP in conservative mandate | Product likely unsuitable |
| Commodity-linked note sold as “safe coupon” | Coupon is compensation for embedded risk |
| Unallocated gold treated as physical ownership | Provider credit risk may be ignored |
| Futures-based product held long term without roll understanding | Return may diverge from spot view |
| ETN issuer concentration ignored | Commodity exposure plus issuer credit exposure |

## 9. Advisor script

“Commodity exposure can help diversify a portfolio and express views on inflation, real assets or specific supply-demand cycles. However, the product wrapper is critical. Physical gold, a gold ETF, an oil futures fund, a commodity ETN and a commodity-linked structured note all behave differently. We need to evaluate volatility, liquidity, fees, roll yield, issuer/counterparty risk, custody, leverage and whether the product fits your risk profile, time horizon and portfolio objective.”

## 10. DPM implementation checklist

- Define allowed commodity families.
- Define allowed wrappers.
- Maintain approved product list.
- Apply concentration and issuer limits.
- Apply derivative/leverage restrictions.
- Calculate look-through exposure.
- Exclude margin-triggering products unless mandate permits.
- Stress test commodity shocks.
- Monitor liquidity and trading spreads.
- Report physical/custody/provider risks clearly.
