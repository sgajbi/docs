# 01 — Funds Fundamentals and Product Taxonomy

## 1. What Is a Fund?

A **fund** is a pooled investment vehicle where money from many investors is combined and invested according to a defined investment objective, strategy and mandate.

The investor usually owns **units** or **shares** of the fund. The fund owns the underlying portfolio.

```text
Investor owns fund units/shares
Fund owns portfolio assets
Fund manager manages the portfolio
NAV reflects the value of the portfolio net of liabilities and expenses
```

A fund can invest in:

- Equities
- Bonds
- Money market instruments
- Deposits
- Derivatives
- Commodities or commodity-linked instruments
- Real estate assets or REITs
- Private equity
- Private credit
- Infrastructure
- Multi-asset portfolios
- Other funds

From a wealth-management perspective, funds are core building blocks for advisory, discretionary portfolio management, model portfolios, retirement planning, diversification, regular savings plans, risk profiling and portfolio reporting.

---

## 2. Why Funds Exist

Funds exist because they allow investors to access a professionally managed portfolio without directly buying every underlying security.

| Investor Need | How Funds Help |
|---|---|
| Diversification | Fund holds many underlying assets. |
| Professional management | Portfolio manager handles research, selection, allocation and rebalancing. |
| Access | Investor can access markets or strategies that may be difficult to buy directly. |
| Operational simplicity | One fund position can represent a diversified portfolio. |
| Smaller ticket size | Investor can participate with smaller amount than direct portfolio construction. |
| Liquidity | Many open-ended funds allow subscriptions/redemptions on dealing days. |
| Portfolio construction | Advisors can use funds as building blocks by asset class, geography, sector, style or risk level. |

---

## 3. Fund vs Direct Security Ownership

| Topic | Direct Security | Fund |
|---|---|---|
| What client owns | Security such as bond/equity | Fund units/shares |
| Who manages selection | Client/advisor/portfolio manager | Fund manager |
| Valuation | Market price of each security | Fund NAV or exchange price |
| Income | Dividends/coupons directly from securities | Fund distributions, if any |
| Corporate actions | Client receives security-level corporate actions | Fund handles underlying corporate actions internally |
| Look-through | Not applicable, directly held | Optional analytics based on fund holdings |
| Cost | Brokerage/custody/security-level costs | Fund expenses, sales charges, platform fees, transaction costs |

Important platform rule:

```text
A fund is a security position in the client portfolio.
The fund's underlying holdings are analytical look-through data unless the client has direct legal ownership.
```

## 4. Fund Structure: Open-Ended vs Closed-Ended

### 4.1 Open-Ended Funds

Open-ended funds issue and redeem units based on investor demand. Investors usually subscribe or redeem directly with the fund or through a distributor/platform.

Common examples:

- Mutual funds
- Unit trusts
- UCITS funds
- Most daily-dealing funds
- Some ETFs, from creation/redemption perspective

Typical characteristics:

| Feature | Open-Ended Fund |
|---|---|
| Units/shares | Created or cancelled based on subscriptions/redemptions |
| Pricing | Based on NAV, usually once per dealing day |
| Trading | Via fund platform, transfer agent, bank or distributor |
| Liquidity | Fund liquidity depends on dealing frequency and underlying assets |
| Investor order | Usually forward-priced at next available NAV |
| Main operational complexity | Cut-off time, NAV date, settlement date, swing pricing, redemption gates |

### 4.2 Closed-Ended Funds

Closed-ended funds issue a fixed number of shares, often through an initial offering. Investors normally trade shares in the secondary market rather than redeeming directly from the fund.

Common examples:

- Listed closed-end funds
- Investment trusts
- Listed private credit funds
- Listed real estate or infrastructure funds
- Some interval funds or tender-offer funds with periodic liquidity

Typical characteristics:

| Feature | Closed-Ended Fund |
|---|---|
| Units/shares | Usually fixed after launch |
| Pricing | Market price may trade at premium or discount to NAV |
| Trading | Usually exchange or secondary market |
| Liquidity | Depends on market liquidity, not daily redemption from fund |
| NAV | Still calculated, but investor exit price may differ from NAV |
| Main risk | Discount/premium risk, leverage, liquidity, market sentiment |

## 5. Mutual Funds and Unit Trusts

A **mutual fund** or **unit trust** pools investor money and invests according to a stated strategy.

In many markets, especially Singapore, the term **unit trust** is commonly used for retail/offshore fund products distributed through banks and platforms. In the United States, **mutual fund** is more common.

Platform-wise, they are usually modelled similarly:

```text
Security type = FUND
Fund structure = OPEN_ENDED
Fund legal form = UNIT_TRUST / MUTUAL_FUND / SICAV / OEIC / FCP / VCC_SUB_FUND / etc.
```

### Common Features

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| NAV-based pricing | Units are bought/redeemed at NAV or adjusted dealing price. |
| Dealing frequency | Daily, weekly, monthly or other dealing schedule. |
| Cut-off time | Orders received before cut-off are processed for a specific dealing date. |
| Settlement cycle | Cash settlement may be T+1, T+2, T+3 or longer. |
| Share classes | Different fee, currency, hedging, distribution and investor eligibility features. |
| Distributions | Some classes distribute income; others accumulate income. |
| Expenses | Management fee, trustee fee, custody, administration, audit and other expenses. |
| Prospectus/factsheet | Defines objective, risk, costs, dealing rules and investor disclosures. |

## 6. Exchange-Traded Funds, ETFs

An **ETF** is an exchange-traded fund. It pools assets like a fund but trades on an exchange like a stock.

ETFs are usually used for:

- Index exposure
- Low-cost beta exposure
- Sector/geography allocation
- Tactical asset allocation
- Portfolio rebalancing
- Cash equitisation
- Model portfolios

### ETF Characteristics

| Feature | ETF |
|---|---|
| Trading | Intraday on exchange |
| Valuation | Exchange price for position valuation; NAV/iNAV for analytics |
| Order types | Market, limit, stop, etc., like equities |
| Units | Traded units/shares |
| Creation/redemption | Usually handled by authorised participants, not normal retail clients |
| Cost | Brokerage, bid/ask spread, expense ratio |
| Liquidity | Depends on exchange trading and underlying basket liquidity |
| Premium/discount | ETF market price can differ from NAV |

### ETF vs Mutual Fund

| Topic | Mutual Fund / Unit Trust | ETF |
|---|---|---|
| Trading | Fund platform, once per NAV/dealing cycle | Exchange intraday |
| Price | Usually next NAV after cut-off | Market price throughout trading day |
| Order handling | Subscription/redemption | Buy/sell exchange trade |
| Settlement | Fund-specific | Exchange market settlement |
| Cost | Sales charge, platform fee, fund TER | Brokerage, spread, fund expense ratio |
| Position type | Fund position | Listed fund/security position |

---

## 7. Money Market Funds

A **money market fund** invests in short-term high-quality instruments such as treasury bills, commercial paper, deposits and short-term government or corporate debt.

Typical uses:

- Cash management
- Liquidity parking
- Low-duration fixed-income exposure
- Alternative to idle cash
- Collateral/cash sweep product

Key features:

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Objective | Capital preservation and liquidity, though not risk-free. |
| Assets | Short-term instruments. |
| Yield | Varies with short-term interest rates. |
| NAV | May be stable or variable depending on jurisdiction/product type. |
| Risks | Credit risk, liquidity risk, interest-rate risk, sponsor risk, regulatory structure risk. |

Important advisory point:

```text
A money market fund is cash-like, but it is not the same as a bank deposit.
```

---

## 8. Bond Funds

A **bond fund** invests in fixed-income securities.

Types include:

- Government bond funds
- Investment-grade corporate bond funds
- High-yield bond funds
- Emerging-market bond funds
- Inflation-linked bond funds
- Short-duration funds
- Floating-rate funds
- Multi-sector bond funds

Bond funds differ from direct bonds:

| Topic | Direct Bond | Bond Fund |
|---|---|---|
| Maturity | Bond has final maturity | Fund usually has no fixed maturity unless target maturity fund |
| Income | Coupon from specific bond | Distribution from pooled bond portfolio |
| Principal | Par repayment if issuer survives and bond held to maturity | No guaranteed par repayment at fund level |
| Duration | Known for bond, changes over time | Fund duration changes with portfolio management |
| Credit risk | Specific issuer | Diversified but still exposed to credit spread risk |
| Liquidity | Bond market liquidity | Fund redemption + underlying bond liquidity |

Common mistake:

```text
A bond fund is not the same as a single bond held to maturity.
```

## 9. Equity Funds

An **equity fund** invests primarily in shares.

Common classifications:

| Classification | Examples |
|---|---|
| Geography | Global, US, Europe, Asia, China, India, ASEAN |
| Market cap | Large cap, mid cap, small cap |
| Style | Growth, value, quality, dividend, low volatility |
| Sector | Technology, healthcare, financials, consumer, energy |
| Strategy | Active, passive, thematic, ESG, factor-based |

Equity fund risks:

- Market risk
- Sector concentration risk
- Currency risk
- Manager risk
- Style drift risk
- Liquidity risk
- Tracking error for index funds

---

## 10. Balanced, Multi-Asset and Allocation Funds

These funds invest across asset classes.

Common types:

- Balanced funds
- Income funds
- Growth funds
- Target-risk funds
- Target-date funds
- Model portfolio funds
- Multi-asset income funds
- Risk-profiled funds

They may hold:

- Equities
- Bonds
- Cash
- Alternatives
- Derivatives
- Other funds

Platform implication:

```text
Asset-class reporting should use fund classification if no look-through is available.
Use holdings look-through only if reliable and dated holdings data is available.
```

---

## 11. Fund of Funds

A **fund of funds** invests in other funds rather than directly holding securities.

Examples:

- Multi-manager funds
- Fund selection portfolios
- Private market feeder funds
- Hedge fund of funds

Key issues:

| Issue | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Double-layer fees | Investor may pay fees at fund-of-funds level and underlying fund level. |
| Look-through depth | Need two-level or multi-level look-through. |
| Valuation lag | Underlying funds may provide NAV with delay. |
| Liquidity mismatch | Fund may offer liquidity that depends on underlying fund liquidity. |
| Concentration | Same underlying exposure may appear in multiple funds. |

---

## 12. Hedge Funds

A **hedge fund** is a pooled investment vehicle that may use more flexible or complex strategies.

Common strategies:

- Long/short equity
- Global macro
- Event-driven
- Merger arbitrage
- Relative value
- Convertible arbitrage
- Credit long/short
- Managed futures / CTA
- Multi-strategy

Key features:

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Liquidity | Often monthly/quarterly with notice period. |
| Lock-up | Investor may be unable to redeem for a period. |
| Fees | Management fee + performance fee are common. |
| Valuation | May involve estimates, side pockets and lagged NAV. |
| Leverage | May use leverage and derivatives. |
| Transparency | Holdings may be less transparent. |
| Eligibility | Often restricted to professional/accredited investors. |

Platform implication:

Hedge funds should not be treated like daily liquid mutual funds. Their transaction lifecycle and valuation lifecycle require notice periods, redemption gates, lock-ups, holdbacks, estimates and final NAV adjustments.

---

## 13. Private Market Funds

Private market funds invest in non-public assets.

Common types:

- Private equity funds
- Venture capital funds
- Private credit funds
- Real estate funds
- Infrastructure funds
- Secondaries funds
- Co-investment funds

Key features:

| Feature | Description |
|---|---|
| Commitment | Investor commits capital upfront, not all paid immediately. |
| Capital calls | Fund calls capital over time. |
| Distributions | Fund returns capital/profits over time. |
| NAV lag | Valuation may be quarterly and delayed. |
| Illiquidity | Long lock-up, limited secondary market. |
| J-curve | Early returns may be negative due to fees and deployment timing. |
| Vintage year | Important performance comparison dimension. |
| Unfunded commitment | Remaining amount investor is obligated to fund. |

Platform implication:

Private market funds require a different position model:

```text
Committed capital
Paid-in capital
Unfunded commitment
NAV / carrying value
Distributions received
Recallable distributions
Realized gain/loss
IRR and multiple metrics
```

Do not force private market funds into a simple units × daily NAV model unless the product is genuinely structured that way.

---

## 14. REITs and Listed Investment Companies

A **REIT** is often traded like an equity, but economically it provides real estate exposure. Listed investment companies and listed closed-end funds can also behave like equities from trading perspective while representing pooled assets.

Platform treatment:

| Product | Trading Model | Economic Classification |
|---|---|---|
| Listed REIT | Equity-like exchange trade | Real estate / equity / income depending on taxonomy |
| Listed investment trust | Equity-like exchange trade | Fund / closed-end fund |
| Listed closed-end fund | Equity-like exchange trade | Fund with premium/discount to NAV |

## 15. Share Classes

One fund can have many share classes. This is one of the most important data-modelling concepts for funds.

A share class may differ by:

| Dimension | Example |
|---|---|
| Currency | USD, SGD, EUR, HKD |
| Distribution policy | Accumulating vs distributing |
| Hedging | Currency-hedged vs unhedged |
| Investor eligibility | Retail, institutional, accredited, clean share class |
| Fee level | A, B, C, I, R, Z classes |
| Minimum investment | Retail vs institutional minimums |
| Load/trailer | Sales charge or trailer fee differences |
| Tax/reporting treatment | Jurisdiction-specific reporting class |
| Registration | Available in certain jurisdictions only |

Important modelling principle:

```text
The investable instrument is the share class, not only the umbrella fund.
```

Example:

| Level | Example |
|---|---|
| Fund house | BlackRock |
| Umbrella | BlackRock Global Funds |
| Sub-fund | BGF Global Allocation Fund |
| Share class | BGF Global Allocation Fund A2 USD |
| ISIN | Specific identifier for that share class |

Client position should point to the **share-class instrument**, because NAV, currency, fees, eligibility, distribution and hedging are usually share-class specific.

---

## 16. Accumulating vs Distributing Share Classes

| Share Class Type | Meaning | Transaction Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Accumulating | Income retained and reflected in NAV | Usually no cash distribution transaction |
| Distributing | Income paid out to investor | Distribution cash transaction or reinvestment transaction |

Accumulating share classes are simpler for cash lifecycle but require correct NAV performance treatment.

Distributing share classes require correct classification of distribution type: income, capital gain, return of capital, equalisation, tax withholding and reinvestment.

---

## 17. Hedged vs Unhedged Share Classes

A fund may have currency-hedged share classes.

Example:

- Fund base currency: USD
- Portfolio assets: Global equities
- Share class: SGD hedged

The hedged share class attempts to reduce currency exposure between the share-class currency and the fund’s base or portfolio currency.

Important limitations:

- Hedging is usually not perfect.
- It can create hedge cost or hedge gain.
- It may hedge only certain currency exposures.
- NAV and returns can differ materially between hedged and unhedged classes.

Platform implication:

Do not assume two share classes of the same fund have the same return. Each share class has its own NAV history and distribution history.

---

## 18. Active vs Passive Funds

| Feature | Active Fund | Passive / Index Fund |
|---|---|---|
| Objective | Outperform benchmark or achieve strategy objective | Track benchmark/index |
| Manager role | Security selection and allocation | Replicate or sample index |
| Cost | Usually higher | Usually lower |
| Risk | Manager risk, style drift | Tracking error, index concentration |
| Performance measure | Alpha, relative return, information ratio | Tracking difference, tracking error |

---

## 19. Key Fund Risks

| Risk | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Market risk | NAV falls due to underlying asset movement. |
| Credit risk | Bond/credit fund holdings may default or widen in spread. |
| Interest-rate risk | Bond fund NAV falls when yields rise. |
| Liquidity risk | Fund may not easily sell assets to meet redemptions. |
| Redemption risk | Large redemptions may force asset sales or gates. |
| Currency risk | Fund/share class currency differs from investor base currency. |
| Manager risk | Portfolio manager underperforms or changes strategy. |
| Tracking error | Passive fund deviates from benchmark. |
| Leverage risk | Leverage amplifies gains/losses. |
| Derivative risk | Derivatives create counterparty, margin, valuation and complexity risk. |
| Concentration risk | Fund concentrated in issuer, sector, geography or style. |
| Valuation risk | Illiquid holdings may be estimated or stale. |
| Operational risk | NAV errors, late prices, transfer-agent errors, corporate action issues. |
| Tax risk | Distribution and withholding treatment may affect client return. |

---

## 20. Summary Mental Model

```text
A fund is a pooled investment product.
Client owns units/shares of the fund.
The fund owns the underlying assets.
NAV represents fund value per unit/share.
Return comes from NAV movement and distributions.
Fund lifecycle is driven by subscriptions, redemptions, switches, transfers, distributions and valuation cycles.
Platform design must separate accounting position, look-through exposure, cash lifecycle and fund-level economics.
```
