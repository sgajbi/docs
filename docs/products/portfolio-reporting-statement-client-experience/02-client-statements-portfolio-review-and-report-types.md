# 02 - Client Statements, Portfolio Review and Report Types

## 1. Client statement

A client statement is the official periodic summary of account activity and holdings. It is often used by clients, advisors, operations and auditors as the primary view of what happened during a reporting period.

Typical sections:

| Section | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Statement header | Client, account, reporting period, valuation date, base currency. |
| Portfolio summary | Total market value, cash, investments, liabilities, net wealth. |
| Asset allocation | Asset class, currency, geography, sector, product type. |
| Holdings | Position-level quantity, cost, price, market value, P&L, income. |
| Cash balances | Opening/closing cash by currency and account. |
| Transactions | Trades, transfers, income, fees, tax, corporate actions. |
| Income summary | Dividends, coupons, interest, distributions and other income. |
| Fees and charges | Advisory, custody, product, transaction and financing charges. |
| Performance | Period return, YTD, since inception, benchmark comparison where applicable. |
| Disclosures | Valuation, risk, product, tax and regulatory disclaimers. |

A statement should be stable, auditable and explainable. It should not be overloaded with every advanced analytics view unless the client segment and mandate require it.

## 2. Portfolio review pack

A portfolio review pack is more advisory than a statement. It supports a client meeting and should explain portfolio quality, alignment, risk and recommended actions.

Typical sections:

| Section | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Executive summary | Key changes, performance, risks, recommended discussion points. |
| Goal/mandate alignment | Current portfolio versus client objectives or mandate. |
| Asset allocation review | Current allocation versus target/model allocation. |
| Performance review | Absolute and relative performance. |
| Contribution analysis | What contributed to or detracted from return. |
| Risk review | Concentration, volatility, drawdown, leverage, liquidity. |
| Income and cashflow review | Income received, expected income, liquidity needs. |
| Product review | Complex products, maturity events, upcoming calls, illiquid assets. |
| Action plan | Rebalance, switch, hedge, reduce concentration, deploy cash. |
| Appendix | Detailed holdings, transactions, methodology and disclosures. |

## 3. DPM mandate report

For discretionary portfolio management, reporting should show whether the manager acted within the mandate and how the portfolio performed against relevant targets.

Required concepts:

| Concept | Reporting treatment |
|---|---|
| Mandate name | Balanced, income, growth, conservative, bespoke, etc. |
| Investment objective | Income, capital preservation, growth, total return. |
| Benchmark | Strategic benchmark or custom benchmark. |
| Target allocation | Strategic asset allocation and permitted ranges. |
| Current allocation | Actual exposure and drift. |
| Breaches | Active, resolved, technical and client-approved exceptions. |
| Performance | Portfolio versus benchmark and peer/composite where relevant. |
| Attribution | Allocation, selection, currency, rates, credit, residual effects. |
| Turnover | Trading activity and portfolio changes. |
| Risk metrics | Volatility, drawdown, tracking error, VaR, concentration, liquidity. |

A DPM report is not just a performance report. It is also evidence of mandate governance.

## 4. Advisory proposal report

An advisory proposal report compares the current portfolio with proposed changes.

Typical sections:

| Section | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Current portfolio | Baseline allocation, risk, performance, liquidity. |
| Proposed portfolio | Expected allocation after trades. |
| Trade list | Buy/sell/switch/subscribe/redeem orders. |
| Rationale | Why each change is recommended. |
| Suitability results | Product/client fit, risk profile, knowledge, concentration, liquidity. |
| Costs and charges | Transaction costs, product fees, exit charges, FX costs. |
| Scenario analysis | Upside/downside, stress, income impact. |
| Client approval | Consent status, timestamps, advisor notes. |

## 5. Risk report

Risk reports may be client-facing or internal.

Common views:

- asset-class exposure;
- issuer exposure;
- counterparty exposure;
- sector/country/currency exposure;
- single-name concentration;
- product complexity exposure;
- liquidity bucket;
- credit rating exposure;
- duration/DV01;
- equity beta;
- derivatives Greeks;
- VaR/CVaR;
- drawdown and stress scenarios;
- leverage and collateral utilization.

Client-facing risk reports should translate technical metrics into understandable implications.

## 6. Performance and attribution report

Performance reports should show return, period, currency, methodology and benchmark.

Core fields:

| Field | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Return type | TWR, MWR, Modified Dietz, simple return. |
| Period | MTD, QTD, YTD, 1Y, 3Y, 5Y, SI, custom. |
| Currency | Portfolio base/reporting currency. |
| Net/gross | Net of fees, gross of fees, gross of tax, etc. |
| Benchmark | Linked benchmark or custom reference. |
| Contribution | Return contribution by asset class/product/security. |
| Attribution | Allocation, selection, interaction, currency and residual. |
| Data quality | Missing values, estimated prices, partial history. |

## 7. Income report

Income reporting is important for yield-oriented clients.

Include:

- income received;
- income accrued;
- projected income;
- income by product type;
- income by currency;
- tax withheld;
- net income;
- yield on market value;
- yield on cost;
- recurring versus one-off income;
- income reliability caveats.

Do not present structured-note coupons, REIT distributions, private fund distributions or insurance benefits as equivalent without qualification.

## 8. Tax-aware report

Tax-aware reporting is not the same as tax advice.

It may include:

- gross income;
- tax withheld;
- net income;
- realized gains/losses;
- tax-lot details;
- corporate-action cost adjustments;
- jurisdiction-specific tax flags;
- CRS/FATCA documentation status;
- tax voucher references.

Platforms should make tax treatment configurable by jurisdiction, client tax status and product type.

## 9. Exception report

Exception reporting should be both operational and client-aware.

Examples:

| Exception | Client-facing treatment |
|---|---|
| Missing price | Holding excluded from market value or valued at last available price with flag. |
| Stale NAV | Mark as estimated/stale and show valuation date. |
| Unconfirmed trade | Show pending activity or exclude based on report basis. |
| Corporate action pending | Show event note and provisional quantity/cash if applicable. |
| Private market NAV lag | Show latest available NAV date and estimated status. |
| Structured product observation pending | Show next observation and pending payoff if confirmed. |

## 10. Management and business reports

Internal reports may aggregate:

- AUM/AUA;
- net new money;
- product penetration;
- revenue and fees;
- advisory proposal conversion;
- DPM mandate drift;
- risk concentrations;
- exception backlog;
- pricing completeness;
- statement production status.

These reports should use governed metrics, not ad hoc spreadsheet definitions.
