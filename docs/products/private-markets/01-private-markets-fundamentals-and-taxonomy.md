# 01 — Private Markets Fundamentals and Taxonomy

## 1. What are private markets?

Private markets refer to investments that are not primarily traded on public exchanges. They include private equity, venture capital, private credit, private real estate, infrastructure, natural resources, hedge funds, co-investments, secondaries and other alternative strategies.

In public markets, an investor usually buys a listed security with observable prices, daily liquidity and standard settlement. In private markets, the investor usually commits capital to a fund, partnership, feeder vehicle or private placement and receives periodic NAV reporting, capital calls and distributions over a multi-year life.

Simple definition:

> Private markets are investments where access, pricing, liquidity, lifecycle and reporting are controlled by private agreements rather than daily public exchange trading.

## 2. Private markets versus public markets

| Dimension | Public equities/bonds/funds | Private markets/alternatives |
|---|---|---|
| Access | Broadly available | Often restricted to qualified, accredited, professional, expert or eligible investors |
| Pricing | Daily exchange price or NAV | Periodic NAV, valuation reports, appraisals or manager estimates |
| Liquidity | Daily/near-daily for many instruments | Lock-up, capital calls, redemption windows, gates or no ordinary liquidity |
| Position | Units, shares, nominal | Commitment, funded capital, NAV, unfunded commitment |
| Cashflows | Buy/sell/dividend/coupon | Capital calls, distributions, recallable capital, fees, carried interest |
| Reporting | Daily market value | Monthly/quarterly NAV, often lagged |
| Performance | TWR and money-weighted return | IRR, TVPI, DPI, RVPI, PME, vintage-year analysis |
| Risk | Market, credit, FX, rates | Illiquidity, valuation uncertainty, manager selection, leverage, vintage, concentration, cash-call risk |
| Documentation | Prospectus, factsheet, exchange data | PPM, LPA, subscription agreement, side letters, capital call notices, distribution notices |
| Operational complexity | Standardised | Highly document-driven and fund-specific |

## 3. Why wealth clients use private markets

Private markets are used in wealth portfolios for several reasons:

| Objective | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Return enhancement | Potential access to companies/assets before public listing or outside public markets |
| Diversification | Exposure to return drivers different from listed equities and bonds |
| Illiquidity premium | Investors may expect compensation for locking capital for years |
| Income | Private credit and real estate may provide distribution-oriented income |
| Inflation linkage | Real assets/infrastructure may have contractual or real-economy cashflow linkage |
| Thematic access | Technology, healthcare, energy transition, infrastructure, real estate, private credit |
| Manager alpha | Specialist managers may source, structure and improve investments directly |
| UHNW access | Co-investments and club deals can provide differentiated access |

However, these benefits are not guaranteed. The same features that make private markets attractive also create complexity:

- difficult valuation;
- uncertain timing of cashflows;
- limited liquidity;
- high fees;
- manager dispersion;
- leverage and covenant risk;
- legal-document dependency;
- tax complexity;
- less transparent reporting;
- operationally intensive lifecycle management.

## 4. Core product families

| Product family | Main return driver | Typical liquidity | Key platform complexity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Private equity | Company growth, operational improvement, exit multiple | 7-12+ year fund life | Commitments, capital calls, distributions, valuation lag |
| Venture capital | Early-stage company growth | 8-12+ year fund life | High dispersion, write-offs, long J-curve |
| Growth equity | Scaling private companies | 5-10+ year horizon | Valuation updates, follow-ons, exit timing |
| Buyout | Control investment in mature private companies | 7-12 years | Leverage, operational value creation, exit multiples |
| Private credit | Contractual interest, fees, credit spread | 3-8+ years | Accruals, amortisation, defaults, covenant tracking |
| Distressed/special situations | Recovery, restructuring, event-driven gains | Opportunistic/illiquid | Mark-downs, restructurings, legal outcomes |
| Real estate | Rent, appreciation, development gains | Illiquid or semi-liquid | Appraisal NAV, property-level metrics, leverage |
| Infrastructure | Long-term operating cashflows | Long-dated | Concession terms, inflation linkage, regulatory risk |
| Natural resources | Commodity-linked real assets | Illiquid/cyclical | Commodity sensitivity, depletion, capex |
| Hedge funds | Manager strategy/alpha, long-short, macro, relative value | Monthly/quarterly with notice and gates | Share-class NAV, liquidity terms, leverage, derivatives |
| Co-investments | Direct deal alongside a sponsor | Deal-specific | Deal-level position tracking and concentration |
| Secondaries | Purchase of existing fund interests | Illiquid but earlier cashflows | Secondary price vs NAV, transferred commitments |
| Private placements | Direct non-public securities | Varies | Suitability, due diligence, illiquidity, docs |

## 5. Private-market structures in wealth platforms

Private-market access can be delivered through different wrappers:

| Wrapper | Example | Platform treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Limited partnership fund | PE fund LP interest | Commitment + capital calls + NAV |
| Feeder fund | Bank feeder into master private fund | Fund position plus underlying private fund exposure |
| SICAV/sub-fund | Luxembourg private markets fund | Fund/security master + commitment terms |
| Closed-end fund | Private credit interval/closed vehicle | Fund position with limited liquidity |
| Evergreen fund | Open-ended private market fund | NAV subscription/redemption with gates and queues |
| Co-investment SPV | Single deal SPV | Direct private investment with deal-level analytics |
| Direct private placement | Private company share/note | Security-like position but illiquid and document-specific |
| Structured note/certificate | Note linked to private market index/fund | Structured product with private-market reference exposure |
| Listed alternative vehicle | Listed PE/REIT/BDC/infra company | Public security with alternative exposure |

The same underlying economic exposure can therefore appear in very different platform forms. A system must distinguish:

```text
Legal wrapper != economic exposure != liquidity behaviour != valuation source
```

## 6. Important private-market vocabulary

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Commitment | Amount investor legally commits to fund |
| Capital call / drawdown | Request from fund manager for investor to contribute part of commitment |
| Funded commitment / paid-in capital | Capital already contributed |
| Unfunded commitment | Commitment not yet called |
| Recallable distribution | Distribution that can be called again later |
| NAV / residual value | Estimated current value of investor’s remaining interest |
| Distribution | Cash or securities returned to investor |
| DPI | Distributions to paid-in capital |
| RVPI | Residual value to paid-in capital |
| TVPI | Total value to paid-in capital = DPI + RVPI |
| IRR | Money-weighted return based on timing of cashflows and NAV |
| Vintage year | Year of fund launch/first investment/first close, used for comparison |
| J-curve | Early negative performance due to fees/costs before investments mature |
| GP | General Partner / fund manager |
| LP | Limited Partner / investor |
| LPA | Limited Partnership Agreement |
| PPM | Private Placement Memorandum |
| Carry / carried interest | Manager performance incentive share |
| Preferred return / hurdle | Return threshold before carry is paid |
| Catch-up | Waterfall stage where GP receives distributions to catch up after hurdle |
| Clawback | Mechanism to return overpaid carry if final economics require it |
| Side letter | Investor-specific negotiated terms |
| Subscription line | Credit facility used by fund to bridge capital calls |
| Recycling | Reuse of returned capital within allowed period/limits |
| Gate | Limit on redemptions |
| Lock-up | Period during which investor cannot redeem |
| Notice period | Advance notice required for redemption |

## 7. Core lifecycle comparison

### Public equity lifecycle

```text
Order -> trade -> settlement -> daily valuation -> dividends/corporate actions -> sale
```

### Private-market fund lifecycle

```text
Fundraising -> subscription/commitment -> closings -> capital calls -> investments -> NAV updates -> distributions -> exits -> liquidation
```

### Hedge fund lifecycle

```text
Subscription -> NAV unit allocation -> monthly/quarterly NAV -> incentive fee crystallisation -> redemption notice -> redemption settlement
```

### Evergreen semi-liquid fund lifecycle

```text
Subscription window -> NAV acceptance -> lock-up -> periodic redemption window -> queue/gate if overloaded -> ongoing NAV
```

## 8. Why private markets are hard for wealth platforms

Private markets challenge normal platform assumptions:

| Normal platform assumption | Private-market reality |
|---|---|
| Position is fully funded on trade date | Commitment may be called over years |
| Market value is daily | NAV may be quarterly and lagged |
| Sell is available any day | Transfer/redemption may be restricted |
| Cashflows are predictable | Calls and distributions are uncertain |
| Product has clean unit price | May have equalisation, side letters, series accounting |
| Exposure equals market value | Exposure includes unfunded commitment and look-through |
| Performance can rely on TWR only | IRR and multiple metrics are essential |
| Data comes from market data feeds | Data comes from notices, PDFs, administrators, portals, manual ops |
| Position close is simple | Final liquidation, escrow, clawback or residual tail may persist |

## 9. Summary mental model

Think of private markets as a combination of:

```text
Long-term legal commitment
+ periodic cashflow obligations
+ manager-reported valuation
+ illiquidity and transfer restrictions
+ delayed investment realisation
+ complex performance measurement
```

For advisory and reporting, the core question is:

> Can the client tolerate the size, timing uncertainty, illiquidity and valuation uncertainty of the investment, not just its expected return?
