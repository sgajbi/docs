# 01 — Real Estate, REITs and Infrastructure Fundamentals and Taxonomy

## 1. What this product family covers

Real estate, REITs, infrastructure and real-asset products give investors exposure to physical or operating assets that may generate income and long-term capital appreciation.

The core theme is ownership or financing of assets such as:

- offices;
- shopping malls;
- logistics warehouses;
- data centres;
- residential property;
- hotels and hospitality assets;
- healthcare properties;
- student housing;
- self-storage;
- industrial facilities;
- airports, roads and ports;
- utilities and power grids;
- renewable energy assets;
- telecom towers;
- pipelines and midstream energy infrastructure;
- social infrastructure such as schools, hospitals and public facilities.

The same exposure can be delivered through many wrappers.

| Wrapper | Example | Operational behaviour | Economic behaviour |
|---|---|---|---|
| Listed REIT | Singapore industrial REIT | Exchange-traded equity-like security | Rental income, property values, leverage |
| REIT ETF | Basket of REITs | ETF/fund | Diversified REIT exposure |
| Property company | Developer/landlord stock | Listed equity | Corporate earnings, development risk |
| Business trust | Infrastructure/business trust | Listed trust | Operating asset cashflows |
| Private real estate fund | Core property fund | Private fund | Capital calls, NAV lag, property appraisals |
| Infrastructure fund | Renewable/transport fund | Private or listed fund | Long-duration operating cashflows |
| Direct property | Apartment/commercial building | Direct asset | Rent, valuation, debt, expenses |
| Structured product | Note linked to REIT index | Structured note | Payoff linked to underlying index/REIT basket |
| Real estate debt | Mortgage/private credit | Loan/bond/private credit | Interest, collateral, default risk |

## 2. Why wealth clients invest in real assets

| Client objective | How product family may help | Important caution |
|---|---|---|
| Income | REIT distributions, property rent, infrastructure cashflows | Distributions are not guaranteed and can be cut |
| Diversification | Different drivers from equities/bonds | Listed REITs can still behave like equities during stress |
| Inflation sensitivity | Rent escalations, regulated/contracted revenue, replacement cost | Inflation linkage varies by lease/contract and may lag |
| Real-asset exposure | Tangible or operating assets | Valuation can be uncertain, especially for private assets |
| Portfolio yield | Income-oriented allocation | High yield can signal risk, leverage or weak asset quality |
| Long-term growth | Urbanisation, logistics, data centres, renewables | Sector themes can become overvalued or cyclical |
| Liability matching | Stable income for spending needs | Liquidity and distribution stability must be assessed |

## 3. Major product categories

### 3.1 Listed REITs

A REIT is a vehicle that owns or finances income-producing real estate. Listed REITs trade on exchanges and are usually represented in systems similarly to equities, but they require additional analytics for property exposure, distribution yield, leverage, net asset value and interest-rate sensitivity.

### 3.2 Non-traded / unlisted REITs

Non-traded REITs may be registered/public or private but are not listed on an exchange. They can provide real estate exposure but are often less liquid, harder to value and subject to redemption limits.

### 3.3 REIT ETFs and real estate funds

A fund can hold multiple REITs, property securities or real estate-related assets. It introduces fund mechanics: NAV, units, subscriptions/redemptions, expense ratios, distributions and look-through holdings.

### 3.4 Property companies and developers

Property companies may own, develop, manage or operate real estate. They are not always REITs. Their risk may include development risk, landbank risk, corporate leverage, earnings volatility and management execution.

### 3.5 Business trusts and stapled securities

Some markets have business trusts or stapled securities combining units in a trust and shares in another company. These can look like REITs from a client-income perspective but have different legal, tax and distribution mechanics.

### 3.6 Private real estate funds

Private real estate funds usually follow private-market mechanics: commitment, capital calls, NAV updates, distributions, long lock-up, appraisal-based valuation and limited liquidity.

### 3.7 Infrastructure funds

Infrastructure funds invest in assets that provide essential services: utilities, roads, airports, ports, towers, pipelines, renewables and social infrastructure. They may be listed or private.

### 3.8 Direct property exposure

Direct property may be held personally, through a trust/company, or in a wealth reporting system as a non-custodial asset. It requires property-level valuation, rental income, expenses, loans, insurance, taxes and ownership share.

### 3.9 Real estate debt and infrastructure debt

These products are debt exposures backed by real assets or project cashflows. They should be treated closer to fixed income/private credit, but with collateral, loan-to-value, covenant and asset-quality analytics.

## 4. Core economic drivers

| Driver | Meaning | Applies to |
|---|---|---|
| Rental income | Income from tenants | REITs, property funds, direct property |
| Occupancy | Portion of space leased | Real estate assets |
| Lease expiry profile | When leases roll off | Real estate income stability |
| Rental reversion | Rent change when leases renew | Real estate growth/risk |
| Property valuation | Appraised or market value | Private/direct assets, NAV |
| Cap rate | Net operating income / property value | Real estate valuation |
| Net property income | Gross property income minus property expenses | REIT/property funds |
| Gearing/leverage | Debt relative to assets | REITs, funds, direct assets |
| Interest cost | Cost of borrowing | All leveraged structures |
| Refinancing risk | Risk debt cannot be refinanced favourably | Leveraged assets |
| Distribution policy | How much cash is distributed | REITs, trusts, funds |
| Development pipeline | New projects or asset enhancement | Developers, REITs, funds |
| Regulatory framework | Tax/distribution/leverage rules | REITs/infrastructure |
| Concession life | Remaining operating rights | Infrastructure |
| Inflation linkage | Revenue linked to CPI or tariffs | Infrastructure/leases |
| Counterparty quality | Tenant/offtaker/concession grantor strength | Income resilience |

## 5. Asset class classification

A system should not classify all real-asset products the same way.

| Product | Primary asset class | Secondary analytics |
|---|---|---|
| Listed REIT | Equity / Real Estate | Sector, country, income, rates sensitivity |
| REIT ETF | Fund / Equity Real Estate | Look-through to REIT holdings |
| Private real estate fund | Alternatives / Real Estate | NAV lag, commitment, property sector |
| Infrastructure fund | Alternatives / Infrastructure | Contract duration, inflation linkage |
| Listed infrastructure stock | Equity / Infrastructure | Equity beta + infrastructure sector |
| Real estate debt | Fixed Income / Private Credit | LTV, collateral, credit spread |
| Direct property | Real Asset / Non-custody asset | Appraisal, rental income, ownership share |
| Structured note linked to REIT index | Structured Product | Underlying real estate exposure |

## 6. Simple mental model

```text
REIT = listed or trust-based exposure to property income and property values.
Private real estate fund = private-market exposure to property assets with NAV lag and limited liquidity.
Infrastructure = exposure to long-duration operating assets and essential-service cashflows.
Direct property = real asset position with rental, loan, expense and appraisal lifecycle.
```

The platform challenge is to support all four without forcing them into one oversimplified model.
