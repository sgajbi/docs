# 01 — Structured Products Fundamentals and Taxonomy

## 1. What is a structured product?

A structured product is a pre-packaged investment whose return, income, principal repayment or settlement outcome depends on a defined payoff formula rather than only on holding a plain asset directly.

A structured product typically combines:

| Component | Role |
|---|---|
| Wrapper | Legal form held by client: note, deposit, certificate, warrant, fund, policy, OTC contract or exchange-traded product |
| Funding leg | Debt/deposit/collateral component that supports repayment or funding economics |
| Derivative leg | Option, forward, swap, credit derivative or combination of derivatives that creates the payoff |
| Underlying/reference asset | Equity, index, FX, rates, credit, commodity, fund, basket or multi-asset reference |
| Payoff formula | Rules for coupon, participation, conversion, barrier, autocall, redemption or loss |
| Lifecycle schedule | Issue date, fixing dates, observation dates, coupon dates, call dates, maturity and settlement |
| Risk disclosures | Market risk, credit risk, liquidity risk, complexity risk, leverage risk, issuer risk and conflicts |

The legal wrapper determines what the client actually holds. The derivative components determine the economics.

## 2. Simple definition for business users

A structured product is an investment designed to deliver a specific outcome under specific market conditions.

Examples:

- earn a higher coupon if an equity basket does not fall too much;
- receive market upside with full or partial capital protection at maturity;
- receive enhanced yield but accept alternate-currency repayment;
- participate in an index up to a cap;
- take leveraged long or inverse exposure to a market;
- monetize volatility by selling optionality;
- hedge a portfolio exposure using a derivative-like payoff.

## 3. Structured product versus direct investment

| Direct investment | Structured product |
|---|---|
| Client owns the asset directly | Client owns a wrapper whose payoff references the asset |
| Return comes from price change, income and corporate actions | Return comes from formula, coupon, barrier, participation or settlement terms |
| Transparent exposure | Exposure may be conditional, leveraged, capped, buffered, path-dependent or issuer-dependent |
| Liquidity may be exchange-based | Liquidity may depend on issuer, market maker or secondary market |
| Valuation often observable | Valuation may require model or issuer/evaluated price |

Example: buying Apple shares is a direct equity investment. Buying an Apple-linked structured product is exposure to Apple through a contract, not direct ownership of Apple, unless physical settlement occurs.

## 4. Common client objectives

Structured products are usually created around one or more client objectives:

| Objective | Typical structure |
|---|---|
| Enhanced yield | Fixed coupon note, reverse convertible, range accrual, dual-currency investment |
| Conditional income | Autocallable, phoenix note, memory coupon note |
| Principal protection | Principal-protected note, market-linked deposit, protected participation certificate |
| Market participation | Participation note/certificate, index-linked product |
| Downside buffer | Buffered return product, barrier-protected product |
| Tactical exposure | Leveraged certificate, tracker certificate, warrant, structured certificate |
| Hedging | Put option structure, collar, forward, swap overlay |
| Currency conversion yield | Dual currency investment, target redemption forward, accumulator/decumulator |
| Credit premium | Credit-linked note, credit-linked deposit, synthetic credit exposure |
| Volatility monetization | Reverse convertible, autocallable, range accrual, volatility-linked structure |

The right question is not “what coupon does it pay?” The right question is:

> What risk is the client being paid to take, and is that risk suitable within the portfolio and mandate?

## 5. Major wrappers

### 5.1 Structured note

A debt security issued by a financial institution or other issuer. Payoff may be linked to equity, index, FX, rates, credit, commodity or multi-asset basket.

Key risk: issuer credit risk plus underlying/payoff risk.

Typical examples:

- principal-protected note;
- equity-linked note;
- fixed coupon note;
- autocallable note;
- credit-linked note;
- index participation note;
- worst-of basket note.

### 5.2 Structured deposit

A deposit-like product whose return depends on an underlying market variable such as an index, equity, FX rate, interest rate or commodity.

Important distinction:

- It may use the word “deposit,” but market-linked return is not the same as a normal fixed deposit.
- Deposit protection treatment depends on jurisdiction, exact product structure and regulatory rules.
- Early withdrawal may be restricted or costly.

Typical examples:

- equity-index-linked deposit;
- FX-linked structured deposit;
- interest-rate-linked structured deposit;
- capital-protected market-linked deposit.

### 5.3 Structured certificate

A certificate is often an exchange-listed or issuer-listed instrument that provides formula-based exposure to an underlying or strategy.

Typical examples:

- tracker certificate;
- discount certificate;
- bonus certificate;
- outperformance certificate;
- capital-protected certificate;
- leveraged certificate;
- inverse certificate;
- daily leveraged certificate.

### 5.4 Structured warrant

A listed security giving leveraged exposure to an underlying. Commonly issued by financial institutions and traded on exchange.

Typical examples:

- call warrant;
- put warrant;
- index warrant;
- equity warrant;
- currency or commodity warrant.

Structured warrants are not the same as company-issued warrants. They are usually issuer-created investment/trading products.

### 5.5 Accumulator / decumulator

An accumulator is a contract where the client buys an underlying periodically at a pre-agreed strike or discount, subject to knock-out and leverage rules. A decumulator is the opposite: client sells periodically.

Typical use:

- accumulator: build position in stock/FX/commodity;
- decumulator: exit position or monetize view.

Key risk:

- Client may be forced to buy/sell more when market moves adversely.
- Knock-out can stop the structure when favourable.
- Leverage can create large exposure.

### 5.6 Equity-linked investment / equity-linked deposit

A product whose return or repayment depends on one or more equities or equity indices.

Common economics:

- enhanced coupon in exchange for downside risk;
- physical delivery of shares if downside event occurs;
- capped upside;
- conditional protection.

### 5.7 Market-linked investment

A broad term for products linked to market variables.

Underlying can include:

- equity indices;
- baskets of stocks;
- interest rates;
- FX rates;
- commodities;
- credit indices;
- funds;
- inflation indices;
- volatility indices;
- multi-asset strategies.

### 5.8 Structured fund / outcome-oriented fund

Some funds implement structured payoff-like strategies using options, swaps or derivatives inside the fund wrapper.

Examples:

- buffered outcome fund;
- covered-call ETF;
- defined-outcome ETF;
- target return fund;
- capital-protected fund, where available;
- volatility strategy fund.

Platform modelling differs from notes because the client holds fund units and usually values through NAV. Payoff economics may still need advisory and risk disclosure treatment.

## 6. Product taxonomy by payoff objective

| Category | Client objective | Product examples |
|---|---|---|
| Yield enhancement | Earn higher coupon/income | FCN, reverse convertible, DCI, range accrual, autocallable |
| Capital protection | Protect principal at maturity | PPN, structured deposit, protected certificate |
| Participation | Participate in upside/downside of an asset | Participation note, tracker certificate, index-linked deposit |
| Buffer / barrier | Limit or defer downside until threshold | Buffered note, barrier certificate, bonus certificate |
| Leverage | Magnify market exposure | Warrant, leveraged certificate, turbo, leveraged note |
| Inverse | Profit from downside or hedge exposure | Put warrant, inverse certificate, bearish structured product |
| Accumulation/distribution | Buy or sell asset over time | Accumulator, decumulator, TARF-style structures |
| Credit exposure | Earn spread for taking credit risk | CLN, credit-linked deposit, synthetic credit note |
| Rate exposure | Express view on rates or yield curve | CMS note, range accrual, callable range note |
| FX exposure | Convert/hedge/monetize currency view | DCI, FX-linked deposit, target redemption forward |
| Multi-asset | Combine underlyings or asset classes | Worst-of note, rainbow note, basket certificate |

## 7. Product taxonomy by wrapper

| Wrapper | Position representation | Typical valuation source | Key platform issue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Note | Nominal/units | Issuer quote, vendor, model, sometimes exchange | Issuer risk, lifecycle events, payoff rules |
| Deposit | Principal amount | Accrued/estimated value, issuer/bank valuation | Deposit vs investment classification, withdrawal rules |
| Certificate | Units | Exchange price or issuer bid | Listing, market maker, corporate-action-like events |
| Warrant | Units | Exchange price/model | Greeks, expiry, knock-out/zero value risk |
| Fund | Units | NAV | Look-through, TER, dealing calendar, fund expenses |
| OTC derivative | Contract notional | Mark-to-market model/counterparty valuation | Collateral, margin, counterparty risk, confirmations |

## 8. Underlying taxonomy

| Underlying type | Examples | Key risk |
|---|---|---|
| Single equity | Apple, DBS, Tencent | Single-name equity risk |
| Equity basket | Apple + Microsoft + Nvidia | Correlation, worst-of, concentration |
| Equity index | S&P 500, Euro Stoxx 50, Hang Seng | Market beta, index methodology |
| FX | USD/SGD, EUR/USD, USD/JPY | Currency conversion and volatility |
| Interest rate | SOFR, SORA, CMS, yield curve | Rate level, curve, reset risk |
| Credit | Corporate entity, sovereign, credit index | Default, spread, recovery |
| Commodity | Gold, oil, copper | Commodity spot/forward volatility |
| Fund | Mutual fund, ETF, hedge fund index | NAV lag, fund liquidity, strategy risk |
| Multi-asset | Equity + FX + rates + commodities | Cross-asset correlation and complexity |
| Volatility | VIX or volatility strategy | Volatility spike and roll risk |
| Inflation | CPI-linked | Inflation index methodology and lag |

## 9. Key risks

| Risk | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Issuer credit risk | Client depends on issuer to meet obligations. Principal protection does not remove issuer risk. |
| Market risk | Payoff depends on underlying asset levels, returns, rates, spreads, volatility or correlation. |
| Liquidity risk | Many products have limited secondary market; exit price may be poor or unavailable. |
| Complexity risk | Client may misunderstand path dependency, barriers, caps, leverage or worst-of mechanics. |
| Principal risk | Client may lose part or all of invested amount if principal is at risk. |
| Early exit risk | Selling before maturity can produce loss even if maturity protection exists. |
| Barrier risk | Payoff changes sharply if a barrier is touched or observed. |
| Autocall risk | Product may redeem early when favourable, limiting upside or shortening income period. |
| Reinvestment risk | Early redemption forces client to reinvest at possibly lower yields. |
| Correlation risk | Basket/worst-of products depend on relationships between underlyings. |
| Volatility risk | Option value and secondary price can change materially with volatility. |
| FX risk | Investment currency, underlying currency and client base currency may differ. |
| Credit event risk | CLN-style structures can lose value due to reference credit default. |
| Counterparty risk | OTC structures depend on counterparty performance. |
| Tax/accounting risk | Income, capital gain, withholding and tax timing may differ by jurisdiction. |
| Conflict/fee risk | Embedded fees, structuring margin and issuer bid/offer spread may reduce value. |

## 10. How structured products fit wealth portfolios

Structured products can be useful but should be controlled carefully.

Potential roles:

- income enhancement for suitable clients;
- tactical expression of a market view;
- monetizing volatility;
- partial downside protection;
- portfolio hedging;
- currency conversion at a preferred level;
- defined-outcome exposure;
- alternative to directly holding an asset.

Potential concerns:

- concentration across issuers and underlyings;
- complexity relative to client knowledge;
- poor liquidity;
- uncertain valuation transparency;
- overuse in income-seeking portfolios;
- mismatch with mandate risk limits;
- incorrect reporting as “bond-like” when risk is equity/FX/credit-like.

## 11. Platform design perspective

The product should be represented across four views:

| View | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Legal/accounting view | What the client legally holds and what transactions are booked |
| Economic/payoff view | How payoff is calculated |
| Risk/look-through view | What risks and underlying exposures the client has |
| Reporting/advisory view | How to explain exposure, scenarios, suitability, income and downside |

The mistake to avoid is modelling a structured product only by wrapper. A structured deposit linked to equities may have lower legal complexity than a note but can still have meaningful equity and option risk. A certificate may be listed but still have issuer and leverage risk. A structured fund may be valued by NAV but still embed option strategies.
