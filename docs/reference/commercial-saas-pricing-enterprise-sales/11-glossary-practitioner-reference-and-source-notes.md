## 2. practitioner-ready answer

A strong commercial model for wealth software must align product value, buyer priorities, pricing metrics, delivery model, contract terms and operational commitments. Banks do not buy only features; they buy a controlled business outcome with predictable cost, security, support, resilience and exit rights. For a wealth platform, pricing should reflect value drivers such as users, portfolios, reports, calculations, modules, AI usage, countries and enterprise scope. The commercial model must also support procurement due diligence, SLAs, implementation services, customer success, usage metering, renewals and expansion. For wealth-platform suite, I would package the suite around clear enterprise outcomes: portfolio analytics, reporting, advisory, DPM, AI decision intelligence and platform foundation.

## 3. Founder-ready answer

For wealth-platform suite, I would avoid selling a broad collection of apps at first. I would choose a focused wedge such as portfolio review AI, performance/risk analytics API, or advisory proposal and suitability assist. The commercial model should combine a base platform subscription, module fees, portfolio or usage tiers, AI usage allowance, premium support and implementation services. The goal is to create a bank-buyable product with measurable ROI, controlled implementation scope, strong security evidence, clear pricing and a path to enterprise expansion.

## 4. Commercial architecture checklist

- Product catalog exists
- Packages are clear
- Pricing metric is measurable
- Usage events are auditable
- Contract terms map to product entitlements
- SLAs are measurable
- Support tiers are defined
- Implementation scope is controlled
- Renewal process is tracked
- AI usage and cost are governed
- Revenue operations dashboard exists
