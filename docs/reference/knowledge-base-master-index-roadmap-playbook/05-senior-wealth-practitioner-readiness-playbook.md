# 05 - practitioner readiness Playbook for Senior Wealth Roles

## 1. practitioner positioning

For senior wealth technology / platform / BA / architect roles, position yourself as:

> A wealth-platform leader who understands products, portfolio analytics, reporting, advisory, DPM, data quality, enterprise architecture, delivery, and AI-enabled transformation.

## 2. Your strongest themes

Use these themes:

- deep private banking / wealth platform domain
- portfolio analytics: performance, risk, attribution, benchmarks
- reporting and client-review experience
- advisory and mandate understanding
- large-scale migration and replatforming
- data quality and source lineage
- enterprise delivery and production controls
- AI decision intelligence and future platform vision
- bridge between business, technology and operations

## 3. Senior answer structure

Use this answer pattern:

```text
1. Business context
2. Product/platform challenge
3. Key design principles
4. Risks and controls
5. Implementation approach
6. Business outcome
```

## 4. Example answer: wealth platform modernization

> I would start by mapping the business capabilities and identifying which systems own client, product, transaction, position, price and reporting data. Then I would define target domain boundaries, source-of-truth ownership, APIs/events, calculation engines and reporting snapshots. I would sequence the roadmap to protect client reporting and performance continuity, run a parallel comparison, and decommission legacy only after reconciliation and business sign-off. For wealth platforms, I would pay special attention to suitability, DPM mandates, product governance, data lineage and multi-country localization.

## 5. Example answer: AI in wealth

> I see AI as a decision-intelligence layer across advisory, portfolio review, operations and reporting. The strongest use cases are advisor preparation, product explanation, performance narrative, mandate-breach explanation, operations triage and next-best-action support. However, AI must be grounded in approved sources, entitlement-aware, evaluated, auditable and human-supervised. I would not allow autonomous advice or order placement without controlled workflows and approvals.

## 6. Example answer: performance and reporting

> Performance reporting requires accurate holdings, transactions, cashflows, prices, FX and product classification. TWR is appropriate for manager performance because it neutralizes external cashflows, while MWR/XIRR reflects investor experience. For reporting, we need reproducible snapshots, methodology disclosure, exception handling, and product-specific treatment for instruments such as structured notes, private markets and derivatives.

## 7. Example answer: DPM and mandates

> DPM requires model portfolios, mandate rules, allocation bands, restrictions, benchmark-relative analytics, drift monitoring and rebalancing workflows. The platform must identify breaches, explain drivers, support approvals, generate trade lists and provide audit evidence. It should distinguish advisory recommendations from discretionary actions.

## 8. Example answer: structured products

> Structured products combine wrappers such as notes or deposits with derivative payoffs. The client's legal holding may be the note, but risk may come from equity, FX, rates, credit or commodity underlyings. A platform should model the legal position separately from look-through exposure, capture lifecycle events such as coupon observations, barriers, autocalls and maturity, and report issuer risk, liquidity risk and payoff scenarios clearly.

## 9. Example answer: data quality and lineage

> In wealth platforms, data quality is directly linked to client trust. A wrong price, FX rate, transaction classification or product risk rating can affect valuation, performance, suitability and reporting. I would define source ownership, validation rules, reconciliation controls, exception workflows, lineage and data-quality dashboards. For reporting and AI, lineage is especially important because outputs must be explainable and auditable.

## 10. practitioner topic map

Prepare answers for:

- notes, bonds, funds, equities, derivatives
- private markets and alternatives
- structured products
- performance TWR/MWR
- contribution and attribution
- benchmark and composites
- DPM mandates and rebalancing
- suitability and product governance
- reporting architecture
- data lineage and controls
- AI in advisory/reporting
- platform architecture
- migration and country rollout
- cloud and DevSecOps
- security and cyber resilience
- vendor management and bank-buyable software
- commercial model and wealth-platform strategy

## 11. Final positioning statement

> My strength is connecting deep wealth domain knowledge with enterprise platform architecture and delivery. I can discuss products, analytics and reporting with business users, but also translate them into data models, APIs, controls, architecture and delivery plans. With AI becoming central, I see the next generation of wealth platforms as governed decision-intelligence platforms, not just transaction and reporting systems.
