# 01 - Wealth Business Strategy Fundamentals and Market Context

## 1. What wealth business strategy means

Wealth business strategy defines how a firm will serve clients, grow assets, deepen relationships, manage risk, improve advisor productivity, modernize platforms and differentiate in the market.

For a wealth platform, strategy is not abstract. It determines:

- client segments served
- product universe
- advisory model
- DPM/discretionary model
- digital and advisor channel design
- cross-border servicing model
- data and reporting capabilities
- operating controls
- investment in AI
- architecture and vendor choices
- transformation roadmap

## 2. Common strategic objectives

| Objective | Meaning for platform |
|---|---|
| Grow AUM / NNM | Need scalable onboarding, advisory, reporting, product access |
| Improve advisor productivity | Need client 360, portfolio review, AI summaries, workflow automation |
| Improve client experience | Need timely reports, digital access, explainable portfolio insights |
| Reduce operational risk | Need controls, reconciliations, lineage, automation |
| Modernize technology | Need API/event/cloud architecture and decommission roadmap |
| Improve data quality | Need source ownership, golden copy, lineage and DQ controls |
| Strengthen suitability | Need target market, product governance, advisory audit |
| Scale DPM | Need model portfolios, mandates, rebalancing, monitoring |
| Use AI safely | Need RAG, guardrails, evaluation, human approval and audit |
| Reduce cost | Need consolidation, automation, vendor rationalization |
| Support multi-country | Need localization, booking centre rules, regulatory controls |

## 3. Wealth client segments

| Segment | Typical needs |
|---|---|
| Mass affluent | digital engagement, simple portfolios, scalable advice |
| HNW | advisor-led service, portfolio review, product breadth |
| UHNW | family office, structuring, alternatives, complex reporting |
| DPM clients | discretionary management, mandate transparency |
| Advisory clients | suitability-aware recommendations and proposals |
| Execution-only clients | trading access, disclosures, platform usability |
| Family office | consolidated reporting, entities, governance, alternatives |

## 4. Wealth platform strategic tensions

| Tension | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Personalization vs standardization | Clients want tailored service; platforms need scalable patterns |
| Speed vs control | Advisors need fast workflows; banks need suitability and audit |
| Product breadth vs governance | Broad product shelf increases risk and complexity |
| Country autonomy vs global platform | Local rules differ, but core capability should be shared |
| Build vs buy | Strategic differentiation may justify build; commodity capability may be bought |
| AI productivity vs AI risk | AI can accelerate work but must be governed |
| Real-time vs batch | Real-time improves experience but increases cost and complexity |
| Legacy continuity vs modernization | Migration must preserve client reporting and performance history |

## 5. Strategy translated into capabilities

A strategy becomes actionable only when mapped to capabilities.

Example:

| Strategy | Required capabilities |
|---|---|
| "Improve advisor productivity" | client 360, portfolio review, AI assistant, workflow, document generation |
| "Grow DPM" | model portfolios, mandates, drift, rebalance, benchmark analytics |
| "Improve reporting trust" | golden data, performance lineage, exception disclosure, report archive |
| "Scale cross-border" | booking centre controls, product eligibility, localization |
| "Adopt AI" | RAG, evaluation, guardrails, audit, human approval |
| "Reduce cost" | platform consolidation, cloud, automation, decommission |

## 6. Executive framing

A strong strategy statement:

> We will build a scalable, controlled and AI-enabled wealth platform that improves advisor productivity, strengthens client reporting, supports advisory and DPM growth, reduces operational risk and enables multi-country rollout through common data, APIs, analytics and governance.

## 7. Key takeaway

The best wealth technology strategy is business-outcome led, control-aware, data-driven and execution-ready.
